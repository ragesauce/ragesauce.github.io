The four assumption knobs are where the real argument lives: turn them, and watch the welfare-maximizing allocation slide from one pole to the other. The verdict isn't in the model. It's in the priors you feed it.

The assumptions that decide it

These are the things real systems actually differ on — and the things hardest to measure. That's the point.

Incentive elasticity0.45
How much people slack off when they don't keep what they make. High → pooling shrinks the pie. Markets' case.
Planner inefficiency (Hayek)0.35
Waste from a central allocator lacking the local knowledge a price carries. High → planning leaks. Markets' case.
Inequality aversion (η)1.20
Curvature of welfare in wealth — the objective function itself. 0 → only total output counts. High → a dollar to the poor counts far more. Redistribution's case.
Shock severity0.30
Droughts that wipe out the unbuffered. Pooling acts as insurance. Planning's genuine case.

The frontier — welfare across every regime, given your assumptions

A fast aggregate model (Atkinson social welfare over a lognormal distribution whose mean and spread follow the same four knobs) sweeps every redistribution level so you don't have to. The peak is the verdict. Move an assumption knob and watch the peak migrate. The grid above is the agent-based confirmation at your chosen point.

Given your assumptions, welfare peaks at r = 0.56  A mixed economy
Markets generate the surplus; partial redistribution converts it into welfare faster than it suppresses output. The Nordic configuration, and where most of the parameter space lands.

How to read it. Crank incentive elasticity and planner inefficiency → the peak slams left: markets win on every metric, exactly as the command-economy record shows. Now drop them and raise η and shock severity → the peak slides right toward pooling. Across most of the middle of the parameter space the optimum sits at a mixed economy — a market that generates the surplus, with redistribution converting surplus into satisfaction. No setting makes the model neutral; whichever pole "wins" was decided by the knobs, not discovered by the sim. That's the lesson, not a bug.

Honesty note. The frontier curve is an explicit welfare model (Atkinson EDE × incentive-and-Hayek pie shrinkage × shock-insurance term). The grid is a separate Sugarscape-style agent sim that should track it qualitatively, not to the decimal. Two views of the same forces — kept separate on purpose so you can see where they disagree.
